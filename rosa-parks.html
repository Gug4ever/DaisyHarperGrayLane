Her Story

The life of a courageous woman whose quiet strength changed the world.

1913

A Curious Child

Rosa was born in Tuskegee, Alabama. As a little girl, she asked endless questions about the world. She noticed the unfair treatment between Black and White people and was curious about why things were the way they were.

Early Years

Growing Up in Segregation

Rosa moved to her grandparents' farm, where she picked cotton to help support the family. She also learned quilting and sewing from her mother, finishing her first quilt at ten and her first dress at eleven. She experienced segregation first-hand when forced to attend a separate school.

1932–1933

Marriage and Education

Rosa married Raymond Parks, whom she described as the "first real activist" she had met. In 1933, she earned her high school diploma — a rare achievement at the time, as only 7% of Black people in Alabama had one.

1943

Joining the Fight

Rosa joined the Montgomery chapter of the NAACP, serving as secretary. That same year, she had her first confrontation with bus driver James Blake, who ordered her to re-enter through the back door. She refused his route after that.

December 1, 1955

The Day That Changed Everything

After a long day at work, Rosa boarded a bus and sat in the section designated for Black passengers. When the driver told her to give up her seat for a white passenger, Rosa calmly refused. She was arrested — and her quiet strength became a lasting symbol of courage.

1955–1956

The Montgomery Bus Boycott

Rosa's arrest sparked a 381-day boycott of Montgomery's buses. The African American community walked to work, organized carpools, and stood together in peaceful protest. The boycott also launched the rise of Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. as a national leader.

1956

Victory

A U.S. District Court ruled that segregation on Montgomery buses was unconstitutional. This legal victory set a precedent for other segregation cases and marked a turning point in the Civil Rights Movement.

Legacy

An Icon Forever

Rosa Parks remained an icon of the Civil Rights Movement throughout her life. She continued to speak out for justice, attending rallies and encouraging future generations to take action in the fight for equality.

"I would like to be remembered as a person who wanted to be free... so other people would also be free."
— Rosa Parks
