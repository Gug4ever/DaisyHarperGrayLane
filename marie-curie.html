Her Story

The life of a groundbreaking scientist who changed our understanding of matter.

1867

A Thirst for Knowledge

Born in Warsaw, Poland, Marie had an insatiable curiosity from a young age. She asked big questions about how the world worked — why stars shine, how plants grow, and what things are made of. While other kids played, Marie read books and did experiments.

Early Years

A Family of Educators

Marie grew up in a family that deeply valued education. Her mother was a headmistress and her father a dedicated teacher. She was the youngest of five children, a brilliant student who excelled especially in math and science.

1891

Paris and the Sorbonne

At 24, Marie left Poland for Paris to pursue her dream of becoming a scientist. She enrolled at the Sorbonne under the name "Marie," often the only woman in her classes. She worked tirelessly, studying late into the night.

1894–1895

Meeting Pierre

Marie met Pierre Curie through a research project at the Sorbonne. Pierre was impressed by her scientific dedication and intellect. They fell in love and married just a year after meeting, beginning one of science's greatest partnerships.

1898

Discovering Polonium and Radium

While studying a glowing rock called pitchblende, Marie discovered it was far more radioactive than uranium alone. Her research led to the discovery of two new elements: polonium (named after her home country) and radium.

1903

First Nobel Prize

Marie earned her doctoral degree and received the Nobel Prize in Physics alongside Pierre for their work on radiation. She became the first woman ever to win a Nobel Prize.

1906

Carrying On Alone

Pierre was killed in a tragic accident on the streets of Paris. Marie was heartbroken but carried on their work. She became the first woman to teach at the Sorbonne, taking over Pierre's position.

1911

Second Nobel Prize

Marie won her second Nobel Prize, this time in Chemistry, for isolating pure radium. She became the first person to win two Nobel Prizes in different scientific fields — a record that still stands.

"Nothing in life is to be feared, it is only to be understood. Now is the time to understand more, so that we may fear less."
— Marie Curie
