Her Story

The life of a fearless aviator who pushed every boundary.

1897

A Spirited Childhood

Born in Atchison, Kansas, Amelia loved exploring from the start. She spent hours reading adventure stories, climbing trees, hunting rats with a rifle, and sledding downhill with her sister Pidge.

1917

Inspired by Courage

While visiting Toronto, Amelia saw wounded soldiers returning from World War I. She trained as a nurse's aide and worked at a military hospital, where stories from pilots first sparked her interest in aviation.

1920–1921

Learning to Fly

After her first flight at a fair in Long Beach, California, Amelia was determined to become a pilot. She studied aviation intensively and within six months bought her first plane — a bright yellow biplane she named the Canary.

1922

First Record

Amelia set her first aviation record, becoming the first female aviator to reach an altitude of 4,300 meters — a significant milestone in her rise to fame.

1928

Crossing the Atlantic

She flew across the Atlantic from Newfoundland to Wales with pilot Wilmer Stoltz and mechanic Louis Gordon. Though she wasn't piloting, the flight solidified her reputation as a pioneering aviator.

1932

Solo Across the Atlantic

Amelia made history by flying solo across the Atlantic in a red Lockheed Vega. After nearly 15 hours battling strong winds, icy conditions, and mechanical issues, she landed in Northern Ireland. The press dubbed her "Lady Lindy."

1937

The Final Flight

Amelia set out on a 29,000-mile journey around the world. After completing over 22,000 miles, she disappeared over the Pacific near Howland Island. Her disappearance remains one of aviation's greatest mysteries.
