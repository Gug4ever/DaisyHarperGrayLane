Her Story

The life of a visionary who imagined the future of computing.

1815

A Curious Child

Ada was born in London, the daughter of the poet Lord Byron and Lady Byron. Fascinated by numbers and patterns from a young age, she loved puzzles and spent hours trying to understand the world through mathematics.

Early Years

An Extraordinary Education

Lady Byron designed a curriculum focused on mathematics and science — a rare choice for a 19th-century noblewoman. She wanted to shape Ada's mind through logic and discipline, steering her away from the poetic tendencies of her father.

Age 12

Dreaming of Flying Machines

Fascinated by the anatomy of birds, young Ada studied their wings, experimented with materials, and imagined a flying machine. This early blend of scientific thinking and imaginative vision would define her entire life.

1833

Meeting Charles Babbage

Through her tutor Mary Somerville, Ada met inventor Charles Babbage. This encounter changed everything. Babbage was developing the Difference Engine, and Ada quickly grasped the potential of his mechanical calculators.

1835

Marriage and Family

Ada married William King, who became the Earl of Lovelace. Supportive of her intellect, he encouraged her studies. They had three children together: Byron, Anne, and Ralph.

1842–1843

The First Computer Program

Ada translated a French paper about Babbage's Analytical Engine, adding extensive original notes more than twice the length of the paper. In her final note, she wrote an algorithm to calculate Bernoulli numbers — considered the world's first computer program.

1979

A Lasting Legacy

The U.S. Department of Defense named a programming language "Ada" in her honor. It is still used today in aviation, healthcare, transportation, and space exploration — a testament to the lasting influence of her groundbreaking ideas.

"The Analytical Engine weaves algebraical patterns, just as the Jacquard loom weaves flowers and leaves."
— Ada Lovelace
