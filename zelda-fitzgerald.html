Her Story

The life of a Jazz Age icon who refused to be only one thing.

1900

A Southern Spark

Zelda was born in Montgomery, Alabama, the youngest of six children. From an early age she was known for her wild imagination, her fearlessness, and her refusal to follow the rules expected of Southern girls.

Teen Years

The Belle of Montgomery

As a teenager, Zelda was the most talked-about girl in town. She danced ballet, wrote poetry for the school yearbook, and was famous for her daring spirit. She wanted a life that was bigger than her hometown.

1918

Meeting Scott

At a country club dance, Zelda met a young soldier and aspiring writer named F. Scott Fitzgerald. He fell in love instantly and promised her a life of adventure. Zelda agreed to marry him — but only once he became a successful writer.

1920

Becoming a Jazz Age Icon

When Scott's first novel became a bestseller, the two married and moved to New York. Zelda's bobbed hair, bold dresses, and fearless spirit made her famous. The press called her "the first American flapper" — the symbol of a new, freer generation of women.

1920s

Paris, Adventure, and Words

Zelda and Scott traveled across Europe, mingling with great writers and artists. Zelda wrote magazine articles and short stories of her own, sharing her sharp wit and unique voice with the world. She was an artist in her own right.

1927

Dancing at 27

At twenty-seven — an age when most ballerinas retire — Zelda began training intensively in ballet. She practiced eight hours a day and earned an invitation to join a professional ballet company in Italy. She wanted to prove she could create something all her own.

1932

Her Novel

Zelda wrote her novel, "Save Me the Waltz," in just a few weeks. It told a story of a young woman searching for her own identity. The book showed the world that Zelda was not just a muse — she was an author, a thinker, an artist with her own voice.

1930s–40s

Painting and Perseverance

In her later years, Zelda turned to painting, creating colorful watercolors of fairy tales, dancers, and city scenes. Even through illness and difficulty, she kept creating, kept dreaming, and kept finding new ways to express the world inside her.

Legacy

A Voice Reclaimed

For decades, Zelda was remembered only as someone's wife. Today, the world recognizes her as a writer, painter, and dancer in her own right. Her story reminds us that everyone deserves to be remembered for who they truly were.

"She refused to be bored chiefly because she wasn't boring."
— Zelda Fitzgerald
